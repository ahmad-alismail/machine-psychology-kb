# What to Call the Field: "Machine Psychology" vs. "AI Psychometrics" / "Machine Behavior"

> [!info] In plain terms
> Researchers studying the "psychology" of AI haven't agreed on what to call their field. One camp says "machine psychology" and runs psychology-style experiments; another prefers "AI psychometrics" or "machine behavior" and focuses on measuring traits. The labels signal genuinely different emphases — process and experiment versus measurement and trait profiling.

## The Disagreement
Is the right framing for studying LLMs' human-like characteristics a **psychology** (experimental, process-oriented) one, captured by the term *machine psychology*; or a **psychometrics / behavior** (measurement-oriented) one, captured by *AI psychometrics* or the broader *machine behavior*? The naming is not merely cosmetic: it tracks a difference in method (designing hypothesis-driven experiments vs. administering validated inventories and reading off scores) and in disciplinary lineage.

## Position A
**Claim**: The field should be framed as "machine psychology" — studying LLMs by treating them as participants in behavioral psychology experiments.
**Held by**: [[sources/hagendorff-et-al-2024]]; [[entities/thilo-hagendorff]] (who introduced the umbrella term in Hagendorff 2023).
**Arguments**: Psychology's empirical, hypothesis-driven tradition (if construct X, expect behavior Y) and its century of experimental controls transfer well to LLMs. The goal is computational insight into emergent abilities and behavioral *processes*, going beyond performance benchmarks. Psychological vocabulary supplies explanatory "thick descriptions" for black-box systems.
**Evidence**: A synthesis of four research areas (heuristics and biases, social interactions, psychology of language, learning) with worked examples drawn from experimental paradigms.

## Position B
**Claim**: The work is better framed as "AI psychometrics" (a measurement subdiscipline) and sits within the broader "machine behavior" program — not "machine psychology."
**Held by**: [[sources/pellert-et-al-2024]]; [[entities/max-pellert]]. The "machine behavior" umbrella is from Rahwan et al. (2019).
**Arguments**: The tractable, scalable move is to repurpose **validated psychometric inventories** as diagnostic instruments and read off trait scores (personality, values, morality, beliefs) for which "there is no ground truth," mirroring the human ability-vs-trait distinction. Pellert's article pointedly never uses "machine psychology" and explicitly echoes Rahwan et al.'s machine-behavior research program. This keeps the focus on measurement and on documenting biases with real-world consequences, while resisting over-anthropomorphizing "mere prediction machines."
**Evidence**: Zero-shot NLI demonstrations across seven encoder models on five inventories, producing comparable psychological profiles.

## Synthesis
These are overlapping programs with different centers of gravity rather than mutually exclusive rivals. Both reject pure performance benchmarking in favor of *diagnosis*, and both flag prompt-sensitivity and anthropomorphism as core hazards. They diverge on emphasis: machine psychology centers **process and experiment** (controls, manipulations, mechanism-implicating behavior), whereas AI psychometrics/machine behavior centers **trait and measurement** (validated inventories, scale scores, cross-model profiling). [[sources/loehn-kiehne-et-al-2024]] pragmatically adopts "machine psychology" as the umbrella label while drawing its rigor from psychometric test theory — illustrating that the two vocabularies are converging in practice even where their banners differ. The terminological split is best read as a healthy division of labor across one emerging field.
