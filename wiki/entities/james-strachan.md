# James W. A. Strachan

> [!info] In plain terms
> A cognitive neuroscientist who led the most comprehensive head-to-head comparison of AI and
> humans on theory-of-mind tests so far — putting GPT-4, GPT-3.5 and LLaMA2 through a whole battery
> of mind-reading tasks against nearly 2,000 people. His key insight was methodological: only a
> *battery* run *many times* against a real human baseline reveals the subtle, non-human ways an AI
> can succeed or fail.

## Profile

Lead and co-corresponding author of [[sources/strachan-et-al-2024]] ("Testing theory of mind in
large language models and humans", *Nature Human Behaviour* 2024), with senior/co-corresponding
author Cristina Becchio. Affiliated with the Department of Neurology, University Medical Center
Hamburg-Eppendorf and the Cognition, Motion and Neuroscience group at the Italian Institute of
Technology (Genoa). Conceived the study, designed the tasks, collected the GPT and human data,
performed the analyses, and wrote the manuscript.

## Key Contributions

- Led a **comprehensive theory-of-mind battery** (false belief, irony, faux pas, hinting, strange
  stories) comparing three LLMs against a 1,907-person human sample — [[paradigms/theory-of-mind-battery]].
- Showed **GPT-4 matched or exceeded human levels on four of five ToM tests**, failing only faux pas.
- Devised the **faux pas likelihood** and **belief likelihood** control experiments that produced the
  **hyperconservatism** account: GPT's faux-pas failure is an excess of caution that prevents
  committing to the likeliest inference, not a failure of mentalistic inference — and showed LLaMA2-70B's
  faux-pas superiority was *illusory* (an ignorance-attribution bias).
- Added the decisive human control to the Ullman-2023 false-belief perturbations, finding **humans also
  fail half of them** ([[paradigms/false-belief-task]]).

## Conceptual Framework

Treats LLMs as participants in psychology experiments under a **species-fair** comparison (Firestone
2020), insisting on a battery of multiple tests, many independent repetitions, and clearly defined
human benchmarks. Reads model behaviour through the **performance–competence distinction**: a model can
be competent to compute an inference yet perform differently under uncertainty because it does not
compute the inference spontaneously to reduce uncertainty (unlike embodied human agents).
