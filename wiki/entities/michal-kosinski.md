# Michal Kosinski

> [!info] In plain terms
> A Stanford psychologist who studies machines the way psychologists study people. He is best
> known for showing that recent language models can pass the classic "false-belief" tests used
> to gauge children's theory of mind — and for arguing that this mind-reading-like ability may
> have emerged on its own, as a side effect of learning language.

## Profile

Sole author of [[sources/kosinski-2024]] ("Evaluating large language models in theory of mind
tasks", PNAS 2024; preprint arXiv:2302.02083, 2023), written at the Graduate School of Business,
Stanford University. A computational psychologist who pioneers applying psychological methods to
LLMs ("psychology of AI").

## Key Contributions

- Showed that recent LLMs solve false-belief tasks: GPT-3-davinci-003 / ChatGPT-3.5-turbo at 20%
  and **ChatGPT-4 at 75%, on par with 6-y-old children** ([[paradigms/false-belief-task]]).
- Advanced the hypothesis that **theory of mind may emerge as an unintended by-product of LLMs'
  language training** rather than being explicitly engineered — paralleling the human link
  between language ability and ToM.
- Authored an explicit **"Methodological Notes"** charter for psychological testing of LLMs
  (reset-to-erase-memory, collect response distributions, avoid Likert/MCQ formats, novel
  tasks + minimally-altered controls against contamination), captured on [[sources/kosinski-2024]].
- Co-author of Hagendorff, Fabi & Kosinski 2023 (intuitive behavior / reasoning biases emerged in
  LLMs but disappeared in ChatGPT), in the same emergent-capability research line as
  [[entities/thilo-hagendorff]].

## Conceptual Framework

Treats LLMs as participants in psychology experiments, emphasizing the distinction between a
machine *having* a cognitive ability and *behaving as if* it does (Turing's "polite convention";
the Searle Chinese-room and brain-replacement debates) — and argues psychological science is a
needed tool for studying increasingly black-box AI.
