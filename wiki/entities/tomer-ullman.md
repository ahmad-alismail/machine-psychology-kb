# Tomer D. Ullman

> [!info] In plain terms
> A Harvard cognitive scientist who studies "intuitive psychology" — how people (and children)
> reason about what others think, want, and believe. He is best known in this wiki for showing
> that a language model's apparent mind-reading collapses under tiny, common-sense changes to the
> test, and for arguing that AI evaluations should start out skeptical.

## Profile

Sole author of "Large Language Models Fail on Trivial Alterations to Theory-of-Mind Tasks"
([[sources/ullman-2023]]), in the Department of Psychology, Harvard University. Works on
intuitive/core cognition — Theory-of-Mind, naïve physics, and computational models of
commonsense reasoning.

## Key Contributions

- **The ToM-robustness rebuttal** ([[sources/ullman-2023]]): demonstrated that GPT-3.5's reported
  false-belief successes flip to failures under eight directed, principle-preserving perturbations
  of the canonical vignettes — the wiki's canonical "trivial alterations break ToM" result that
  grounds the perturbation layer of [[paradigms/false-belief-task]].
- **Two evaluation principles** for machine intuitive psychology: a *skeptical zero-hypothesis*,
  and *outlying failures should outweigh average success rates* (the multiplication analogy).

## Conceptual Framework

Argues that mental-state attribution is not done "in a vacuum": people infer intelligence from the
*algorithms* likely generating a behavior, not only from input–output — so a machine can pass a
ToM test that remains valid for humans while still being suspect (the "jumping over the horns"
stance, drawing on the Turing-Test critique and Block 1981). Holds that machine ToM is achievable
in principle, favoring the integration of explicit computational ToM models (inverse planning,
naïve utility calculus, ToM as inverse RL) with language models over spontaneous emergence from
text.
