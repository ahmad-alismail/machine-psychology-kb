# CogBench Cognitive Phenotyping

> [!info] In plain terms
> A way to read an AI model's "personality of thought." You run a model through seven classic
> psychology experiments — betting on balloons, exploring slot machines, planning multi-step
> journeys — and from its choices you extract ten numbers describing *how* it decides: how
> reckless it is, how much it explores, how planful it is, how well it knows when it's right.
> Every number is scaled so that 0 = a coin-flipping random agent and 1 = the average human,
> letting you compare any model directly against people. Crucially, the test items are
> generated by procedure each run, so a model can't have memorized the answers.

## Theoretical Grounding

Each task is grounded in a distinct construct from the learning-and-decision-making literature:

- [[theories/bayesian-belief-updating]] — probabilistic-reasoning task; exploits "system
  neglect" (underweighting priors and likelihoods).
- [[theories/directed-and-random-exploration]] — horizon task; dissociates uncertainty-directed
  from noise-driven exploration.
- [[theories/metacognition]] — restless-bandit task; whether an agent can assess its own choice
  quality.
- [[theories/reinforcement-learning-optimism-bias]] — instrumental-learning task; learning rate
  and asymmetric (optimistic) updating.
- [[theories/model-based-vs-model-free-rl]] — two-step task; planful vs. habitual control.
- [[theories/temporal-discounting]] — temporal-discounting task; preference for sooner-smaller
  over later-larger rewards.
- [[theories/computational-phenotyping]] — the meta-framing: describe each model by a vector of
  fitted model parameters.

The BART risk task is grounded in the behavioral risk-taking tradition (Lejuez et al. 2002),
shared with [[theories/cumulative-prospect-theory]] via [[safety-concepts/risk-taking]].

## Target Behaviors

- Detects: [[safety-concepts/risk-taking]] — via the BART sub-task (risk = average inflation
  attempts).
- Characterizes (general behavioral profile, no specific dangerous-capability target):
  probabilistic reasoning, directed/random exploration, meta-cognition, learning rate,
  optimism bias, model-basedness, and temporal discounting. These calibrate *how* a model
  decides — a prerequisite for predicting when it decides badly — rather than naming a hazard.

## Setup / Elicitation

Seven text-based cognitive experiments are administered purely through prompting, relying
entirely on the LLM's in-context learning (no fine-tuning), at **temperature 0** (deterministic
responses). Past behavior is concatenated back into the prompt so learning is induced via
**prompt-chaining**: the model completes a given prompt to indicate each choice. The benchmark
was applied to **35 LLMs** (proprietary: GPT-3/4, Claude-1/2, PaLM-2; open-source: MPT, Falcon,
many LLaMA-2 variants), yielding **ten behavioral metrics** plus a performance metric per task.

All metrics are **human-normalized**: 0 = a random agent, 1 = the average human subject. The
seven sub-tasks (each `task (citation) — target behavior → scoring`):

1. **Probabilistic reasoning** (Dasgupta et al. 2020) — a "wheel of fortune" sets prior
   probabilities and two urns with colored-ball distributions set likelihoods; after a ball is
   drawn the model estimates the posterior probability of the source urn. → **prior weighting β₁
   & likelihood weighting β₂**, fit by least-squares log-odds regression of a generalized Bayes
   rule P(A|B) ∝ P(B|A)^β₂ · P(A)^β₁. Humans show "system neglect" (both weights < 1). 100 sims.
2. **Horizon task** (Wilson et al. 2014) — a two-armed bandit with stationary rewards; four
   forced-choice trials (equal- or unequal-information) precede either 1 or 6 free choices. →
   **directed exploration** (β for the horizon regressor in the *unequal*-info condition) &
   **random exploration** (β for the reward×horizon interaction in the *equal*-info condition).
   100 sims.
3. **Restless bandit** (Ershadmanesh et al. 2023) — a *non-stationary* two-armed bandit
   (N(60,8) vs N(40,8)) that switches the better arm every 18–22 trials; the model also reports
   confidence 0–1 each trial. → **meta-cognition** via adjusted QSR = 1 − (accuracy − scaled
   confidence)² (Carpenter et al. 2019). ~80 trials (4 blocks), 10 sims.
4. **Instrumental learning** (Lefebvre et al. 2017) — four interleaved two-armed bandits (96
   trials each, 24 per machine; symmetric and asymmetric reward conditions). → **learning rate α**
   (Rescorla–Wagner + softmax, min. neg. log-likelihood) & **optimism bias** = α⁺ − α⁻ (separate
   learning rates for positive vs. negative prediction errors). 10 sims.
5. **Two-step task** (Daw et al. 2011) — a sequential RL task (70% common transition, 20-day
   horizon) designed so model-free and model-based agents earn equal average reward. →
   **model-basedness** = the β for the reward×transition interaction on "stay probabilities."
   100 sims.
6. **Temporal discounting** (Ruggeri et al. 2022) — a series of choices between a smaller-sooner
   and a larger-later monetary outcome (gains at two magnitudes, losses, plus anomaly probes:
   present bias, subadditivity, delay–speedup and delay–length asymmetry). → **discounting score
   0–19** (rank of the switch point; 0 = always patient, 19 = always impatient). The only task
   *not* procedurally generated → 1 sim.
7. **Balloon Analogue Risk Task / BART** (Lejuez et al. 2002) — inflate balloons of types A/B/C
   for reward, with a rising pop probability that wipes the balloon's reward; 10 balloons per
   type. → **risk-taking = average number of inflation attempts**. 10 sims.

After collecting metrics, **hypothesis-driven analyses** use UMAP (visualization) and
**multilevel (mixed-effects) regression** of model features (parameters, RLHF, code fine-tuning,
open-source, etc.) onto behavioral/performance metrics — the multilevel structure accounts for
fine-tuned versions nested within a base model. A separate **prompt-engineering ablation** appends
chain-of-thought (CoT) or take-a-step-back (SB) instructions and re-measures targeted behaviors.

## Scoring / Procedure

- Two metric classes per task: a **performance metric** (the score participants optimize) and one
  or more **behavioral metrics** (how the task is solved), all human-normalized (0 = random,
  1 = average human). Six performance metrics across seven tasks (temporal discounting's
  performance metric doubles as its behavioral one).
- Behavioral metrics are recovered by fitting **computational cognitive models** (log-odds Bayes
  regression, Rescorla–Wagner, softmax choice models, stay-probability regressions, adjusted QSR)
  — i.e. the model's choices yield a **computational phenotype** ([[theories/computational-phenotyping]]).
- **Hypothesis testing** via multilevel regression isolates the effect of a single model feature
  by comparing models within the same base-model group.
- **Controls the design requires:** procedural generation of items each run (anti-contamination);
  shuffled symbol/letter assignments (e.g. balloon types, bandit symbols) to avoid letter biases;
  equal vs. unequal information and horizon-1 vs. horizon-6 conditions to *baseline pure
  exploitation* and separate directed from random exploration; default ≥10–100 simulations per
  task; temperature 0 for determinism.

> [!note] BART metric deviation from the canonical scoring
> The canonical BART score is "adjusted risk" — average pumps *excluding* popped balloons. CogBench
> deliberately reports the **average number of inflation attempts including all balloons**, because
> some LLMs exhibit an edge behavior of *always* inflating that the adjusted measure would hide
> (B.7.4). This is a construct-validity choice worth noting when comparing CogBench risk scores to
> the human BART literature.

## Validity Concerns

- **Contamination / data leakage.** The benchmark's headline defense: tasks are "programmatically
  generated, minimizing data leakage concerns," making it "hard to game our benchmark by training
  on the test set." The lone exception is temporal discounting (not procedurally generated, 1 sim).
- **External validity for LLMs is unestablished.** The tasks' external validity is proven *for
  humans* but "it is yet to be shown for LLMs" (Sec 7) — a model could match a human metric without
  the construct generalizing to its real downstream behavior. See
  [[questions/cognitive-task-external-validity-for-llms]].
- **Construct validity via behavioral vs. performance metrics.** Identical performance can mask
  opposite behavior (LLaMA-2-70 vs. -70-chat on risk-taking), and high performance can co-occur with
  *no* human-like exploration ("high performance primarily through exploitation") — so a performance
  number alone misreads the underlying strategy.
- **Reduced protocols.** Trial counts were cut from the human versions (restless bandit ~80 vs. 400;
  BART 10 vs. 15 balloons/type) due to context-size limits, which may weaken the fitted parameters.
- **Regression precision limited by model opacity.** Proprietary models hide features needed as
  regressors, limiting the hypothesis tests' precision.
- **Generation instability under flexible prompting.** In the CoT/SB ablation, allowing flexible
  token output triggers chaotic "Waluigi effect" outputs in some models, hard to revert (App D).

## Evidence

- **RLHF → more human-like (H1).** RLHF'ed models are ~2× closer to human behavior in UMAP; 11.7%
  smaller average L2-norm distance to humans. (Sec 5)
- **Size → performance & model-basedness (H2/H3).** Parameters significantly raise performance
  (β = 0.277, z = 14.1, p < 0.001) and model-basedness (β = 0.481, z = 4.2, p < 0.001); dataset
  size and code fine-tuning are not significant for either.
- **RLHF → meta-cognition (H4).** β = 0.461, z = 5.9, p < 0.001.
- **Open-source models take *less* risk (H5 refuted).** Proprietary models are more risk-taking
  (β = −0.612, z = −11.4, p < 0.001), opposite the pre-prompt-safety hypothesis.
- **Prompt engineering.** CoT raised probabilistic-reasoning posterior accuracy +9.01% (SB +3.10%);
  SB raised model-basedness +118.59% (CoT +64.59%) → CoT best for probabilistic reasoning, SB best
  for model-basedness. (Sec 6)
- **Profiles.** Models weight priors ≫ likelihoods, show pervasive strong optimism bias, and a
  bimodal BART risk profile ("either never take any risks at all or always risk everything"). GPT-4
  and Claude-1 reach human-level on 5/6 tasks; the BART is hard for all. (Sec 4)
