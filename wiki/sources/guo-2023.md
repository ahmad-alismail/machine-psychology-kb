# GPT in Game Theory Experiments

> [!info] In plain terms
> Can a language model play classic economics games the way people do? This paper sits GPT-4 down as a
> player in two famous games — the ultimatum game (split 100 dollars; the other side can reject and
> leave you both with nothing) and the prisoner's dilemma (cooperate or betray) — for five rounds each.
> It finds GPT behaves a lot like humans: it offers fair-ish splits, rejects stingy ones, and
> cooperates when it pays to. By telling GPT to act either "fair" or "selfish," the author shows that a
> single instruction reshapes its play. And because GPT explains itself before every move, the paper
> reads those explanations to learn *why* it acts as it does — uncovering, among other things, that GPT
> sometimes cooperates in the final round for plainly mistaken reasons.

## Relevance Tier

CORE — studies the strategic *behavior* of GPT-4 using the experimental methods of behavioral
economics and game theory. GPT-4 is treated as the experimental subject ("pretend that you are a human
in the game"), run on canonical repeated games, with its behavior compared against human-subject
findings and steered by assigned personality traits. The AI framing is the source's own throughout, so
no wiki-authored bridge is required.

## Relevance to Machine Psychology

*(Optional CORE note.)* The reusable methodological contributions for behavioral AI evaluation are:
(1) **persona-prompted economic games** — running canonical strategic games on an LLM while inducing a
named trait ("fairness concern" vs. "selfishness") with a single-word prompt change, so the
trait→behavior link is the manipulated variable; (2) **elicited-reasoning content analysis** — having
the model emit a reasoning string with every decision, then using an LLM-as-classifier (validated by
manual review) to code those statements into theory-derived categories, which both explains behavioral
patterns and exposes strategic judgment errors; and (3) **human-baseline benchmarking** — reading the
model's offers/rejections/cooperation against established human meta-studies.

## Key Claims

- **GPT rejects backward induction in the ultimatum game.** Proposers offer significantly positive
  amounts in all treatments (p < 0.01). (§3, Table 1)
- **Assigned traits steer offers.** Fair GPT offers ~40% on average (means 38.83 / 40.28), selfish GPT
  ~30% (28.55 / 30.94); difference significant (Wilcoxon signed-rank, p < 0.01). The fair proposer's
  ~40% matches the human meta-study average of 40% (Oosterbeek et al. 2004). (§3, Table 1)
- **Rejection tracks fairness.** Rejection is significantly positive in all treatments except
  fair-proposer/selfish-responder, and highest (~18%) in the selfish-proposer/fair-responder group;
  the source compares this to human studies showing "an average acceptance rate of around 16%" (the
  source's phrasing, while comparing to GPT's rejection rate — an apparent acceptance/rejection
  conflation, quoted as written). (§3, Table 2)
- **Rejection falls with higher offers** (logit coef −0.237***) and with round; **selfish responders
  reject significantly less** than fair ones. Offers decline across rounds (OLS round coef −1.531***)
  and are lower against selfish responders even though proposers are not told the responder's type.
  (§3, Table 3)
- **Round-3 rejection peak.** Rejection is non-monotonic across rounds, peaking in round 3 (near-zero
  in rounds 1 and 5). Reasoning analysis: all 72 round-3 rejection statements cite hope of better
  future offers; ~80% mention a trend of decreasing/less-fair offers. (§3)
- **Prisoner's dilemma: high cooperation only when both fair.** Rate of choosing C = 0.994 (fair-fair),
  0.189 (selfish-fair), 0.091 (selfish-selfish); one selfish GPT largely disrupts cooperation. (§4,
  Table 4)
- **Fair GPT plays tit-for-tat** (advantage + disadvantage aversion): cooperation given DC = 0.750
  (fair) vs. 0.104 (selfish); selfish GPT cooperates ~62.9% only after mutual cooperation, otherwise
  exploiting. Recovery from mutual defection is poor for both, consistent with Akata et al. 2023. (§4,
  Table 5)
- **Cooperation drivers and errors.** Reputation building appears in >95% of first-four-round
  cooperation cases, altruism in <20%. ~80% of the 207 final-round cooperation instances show judgment
  errors (e.g., believing one's own defection could provoke simultaneous defection) — a strategic
  limitation of current GPT. (§4)

## Theories / Paradigms / Instruments Introduced

**Theories** (predictions the design exploits)
- [[theories/inequity-aversion]] — **new**: fairness concern (Fehr & Schmidt 1999); the prediction the
  "fair" persona should exhibit in the ultimatum game (positive offers, rejection of low offers),
  against the self-interested subgame perfect equilibrium.
- [[theories/social-dilemma]] — **existing**: the prisoner's dilemma (T > R > P > S, mutual defection
  the unique Nash equilibrium); the bare payoff structure cooperation must beat. Also draws on
  reciprocity / tit-for-tat (Axelrod & Hamilton 1981) and the reputation-building-vs-altruism account
  of finitely-repeated-PD cooperation (Andreoni & Miller 1993; Kreps et al. 1982; Cooper et al. 1996),
  used as the reasoning-coding categories.

**Paradigms**
- [[paradigms/persona-prompted-strategic-games]] — **new**: two GPT-4 instances play a repeated
  ultimatum game and a repeated prisoner's dilemma, crossed with fair/selfish assigned personas, with
  elicited-reasoning text analysis. A persona-augmented, reasoning-analyzed instance of
  [[paradigms/repeated-games]].

**Instruments**
- [[instruments/guo-game-prompts]] — **new**: the two game scenarios (5-round ultimatum over 100
  dollars; 5-round prisoner's dilemma, 200/100/0/300) and the fair/selfish feature strings, with full
  prompts (Appendix A).

## Limitations

- **Construct validity / induced traits.** Personas are installed by a one-word prompt change; whether
  GPT *has* a fairness disposition or merely outputs fairness-shaped text is unresolved, and the
  elicited reasoning may be post-hoc rationalization.
- **Strategic-competence limitation.** Final-round cooperation is partly driven by judgment errors
  (~80% of cases), indicating a limit in GPT-4's handling of complex strategic interaction.
- **Prompt sensitivity.** The author flags GPT's sensitivity to input prompts; the flexibility enabling
  trait manipulation can reduce robustness (cites Horton 2023; calls for best practices, Hagendorff
  2023).
- **Contamination.** The ultimatum game, prisoner's dilemma, and tit-for-tat are canonical and likely
  in training data.
- **Single model / snapshot.** One model (gpt-4-1106-preview), run Nov 16–20 2023; temperature = 1 for
  play, 100 simulations per treatment (700 total); reasoning classifier at temperature = 0.
