# GPT-ology, Computational Models, Silicon Sampling: How should we think about LLMs in Cognitive Science? (Ong 2024)

> [!info] In plain terms
> This is a short review — a map of the *kinds of questions* cognitive scientists ask when they
> run experiments on large language models. Ong sorts that work into three families: studying
> what the model itself can do ("GPT-ology"), using the model as a stand-in theory of how humans
> think ("LLM-as-computational-model"), and using the model to imitate survey respondents
> ("silicon sampling"). The same experiment can feed any of the three; what changes is the
> conclusion you draw. The paper's real payload is a checklist of the things that can quietly
> invalidate any of these conclusions — memorized test items, brittle prompts, closed models that
> change under you, and randomness that should be embraced rather than switched off.

## Relevance Tier

CORE — a meta-review of the research paradigms used "to make scientific inferences about
'cognition' in these models or about human cognition." Like [[sources/hagendorff-et-al-2024]], it runs no
new experiment, but it is squarely about studying AI/LLM *behavior* with the methods of cognitive
science, and Ong draws the AI framing himself throughout. Its load-bearing contribution for this
wiki is (a) a **typology** of three behavioral research paradigms and (b) a catalog of
**Outstanding Issues** — validity / reproducibility constraints that govern how any of these
behavioral evaluations should be run and interpreted.

## Key Claims

- **One base experiment, three inferences.** The paradigms "are not mutually exclusive; in fact,
  they all rely on the same base experiment of having LLMs provide responses to some input … and so
  the same data can be used to address multiple paradigms." They differ "by their specific research
  goals, and hence the inferences that are made from the results." ("A typology of research
  paradigms"; Fig. 1.)
- **Existence-proof logic is asymmetric.** A model that "can do X" licenses a claim about
  *sufficient* (not necessary) conditions for a capability; a model that "cannot do X" cannot ground
  a necessary-conditions claim because "it could easily be falsified with a newer and more capable
  model." "Arguing from a lack of ability is less scientifically sound than an argument from the
  presence of one." This sharpens [[theories/performance-competence-distinction]]. ("LLMs as
  computational models / Challenges".)
- **Stochasticity is a feature, not a bug.** "A common misconception is that the stochasticity of
  LLMs is a flaw for reproducibility, and some studies are run with temperature set to 0 … This is
  an incorrect perception." Because LLMs (like humans) sample probabilistically, scientists "should
  be thinking about collecting samples from LLMs … and doing statistics over those samples. This is
  currently not common practice." Ideally one measures "the probability distribution of tokens" —
  observable in some open-source LLMs, not proprietary ones. (Outstanding Issue 5.)
- **Prompt brittleness implies overfitting/memorization.** LLM performance is "susceptible to
  seemingly innocuous changes in the stimuli prompts, such as reversing the order of answers … or
  trivial alterations to the stimuli ([[sources/ullman-2023]])." This brittleness "Mathematically … suggests
  that the model is overfitting. Cognitively, it suggests a learnt stimulus-response (memorization),
  rather than true conceptual understanding." (GPT-ology/Challenges; Outstanding Issue 5.)
- **Prompting is "more an 'art' than a science."** Persona prompts and chain-of-thought (Wei et al.
  2022) help, but a bad prompt can cause **false-negative inferences** — a paper's failure to elicit
  X "is because they did not find the 'right' prompt." Remedy: report prompts, procedures, and
  hyperparameters (temperature; access date for proprietary models) in full, and **counterbalance**
  (permute answer choices / information order) as in human studies. (Outstanding Issue 5.)
- **Data leakage can invalidate the whole approach.** If a task such as the "Sally-Anne"
  False-Belief task or Kahneman-and-Tversky-style fallacy items "happens to be in the training data
  … the model might succeed on the task simply from having seen and memorized it." "If we cannot
  guarantee that our tasks were not part of the training data, then a large portion of our
  experimental approach will be rendered invalid." Compounding worry: LLM output re-entering future
  training data, a "Ship of Theseus" for human text. (Outstanding Issue 4.)
- **Proprietary commercial models break the science.** Closed-source models block introspection of
  activations and training data (Frank 2023b); companies update models without past checkpoints,
  which "might render the idea of reproducibility meaningless"; access can vanish for "economic,
  political, or legal" reasons. "Should we really be yoking the success of our science to such
  commercial products?" Undisclosed **guardrails** may also "constrain a model's moral judgments to
  conform to a particular view," and RLHF can induce idiosyncrasies (e.g. GPT adding a happy ending
  to a requested negative story). (Outstanding Issue 3.)
- **Individual vs. population.** Silicon-sampling assumes LLM outputs are "samples from an
  underlying distribution" approximating real human distributions; an alternative view treats output
  as a "population average, or 'aggregate' summary of human knowledge (e.g., a cultural technology
  like a library; Yiu et al. 2023)." "These two conceptualizations are distinct and will affect
  experimental design and inferences" — analogous to probability-matching vs. MAP in Bayesian
  cognitive science. (Silicon Sampling/Challenges.)
- **WEIRD training data.** LLMs are trained predominantly on Western, Educated, Industrialized,
  Rich, Democratic data (Henrich et al. 2010, 2023), and even within WEIRD on "mostly young,
  internet-savvy users." Silicon-sampling of minorities is undercut because those groups "are also
  underrepresented in the training data," risking "(biased) stereotypes … rather than actual
  behavior." (Outstanding Issue 4; Silicon Sampling.)
- **Generalizability/longevity.** Inferences pinned to current models "will be updated and perhaps
  made obsolete in a matter of months"; the field should "consider prioritizing research paradigms …
  that produce more generalizable, lasting knowledge." (Outstanding Issue 6.)
- **Cross-model inconsistency yields engineering, not cognitive, answers.** If "a particular
  'ability' was demonstrated by GPT-4 but not GPT-3," the explanation for the gap "seem[s] like
  [it] would yield engineering answers, rather than cognitive insights." (Outstanding Issue 2.)

## Theories / Paradigms / Instruments Introduced

- Paradigms (NEW from this source — the typology):
  [[paradigms/gpt-ology]], [[paradigms/llm-as-computational-model]], [[paradigms/silicon-sampling]].
  These are **inference frameworks**, not new stimulus designs; their Setup section is the shared
  base experiment, and what distinguishes them is the inference drawn.
- Theory (enriched, not created): [[theories/performance-competence-distinction]] — Ong adds the
  *asymmetric* existence-proof sharpening.
- Nascent paradigms Ong flags but does not develop (documented inline, no pages): LLMs as building
  blocks in neurosymbolic models (Kwon et al. 2023; Zhan et al. 2023); LLMs as research *tooling* to
  generate / classify / annotate stimuli (Demszky et al. 2023; Rathje et al. 2023; Ziems et al.
  2023). ("Other uses of LLMs".)
- Instruments: none introduced. Named only by reference (no pages): World Values Survey, validated
  personality/empathy/autism scales, Winograd Schema, Sally-Anne false-belief items, Kahneman-Tversky
  fallacy items.
- Etymology: "GPT-ology" is coined after **"BERT-ology"** (Rogers et al. 2021).

## Relationship to Other Sources

- The methodological backbone overlaps with [[sources/hagendorff-et-al-2024]] (both are reviews charting how
  to study LLM behavior), but Ong's frame is the **inference licensed**, and his Outstanding Issues
  push harder on closed-source dependence and on embracing stochasticity via sampling.
- His validity catalog restates, from the cognitive-science side, the same failure modes
  [[sources/loehn-et-al-2024]] formalizes as the seven psychometric requirements

- The contamination concern (Sally-Anne / K-T items memorized) is the same data-leakage worry that
  motivates [[paradigms/false-belief-task]]'s and [[paradigms/heuristics-and-biases-battery]]'s
  Validity Concerns.

## Limitations

- A short review/perspective: it introduces no primary data, paradigm of stimulus, or instrument;
  its paradigm descriptions are at survey depth and its claims are syntheses of cited results.
- Deliberately meta: Ong states he "will not comment on specific psychological claims … vis-à-vis
  the literature," focusing on "scientific reliability and validity, logical deductions, and
  epistemic support" — so individual empirical results are illustrative, not adjudicated.
- Offers diagnoses more than fixes: "We do not claim to have answers to all of these issues" — the
  Outstanding Issues are presented to "start conversations," not resolved.
