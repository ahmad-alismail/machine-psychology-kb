# Altruistic Punishment

> [!info] In plain terms
> Sometimes people punish a cheater even though doing so costs *them* money and earns them nothing in
> return. Why bother? Because punishing a norm-violator keeps the group fair and cooperative — a
> benefit everyone shares, including the people who were never wronged. This "I'll pay to make you
> behave" instinct is called altruistic punishment, and it turns out to be one of the engines that
> keeps human cooperation from collapsing. Researchers measure it by giving someone the option to
> spend their own reward to penalize an unfair player.

## Statement

**Altruistic punishment** "refers to the phenomenon where individuals uphold social norms by
punishing others, although the punishment is costly for them and yields no material gain"
(Fehr & Gächter 2002). Although it "may seem irrational at the individual level, it plays a
significant role in maintaining social cooperation and fairness."

Key, testable predictions:

- **People will incur a personal cost to penalize a norm-violator** even when they gain nothing
  material and were not themselves the victim (third-party punishment). The willingness to do so
  scales with the perceived unfairness of the violation.
- **Altruistic punishment sustains cooperation at the group level.** It "occupies an extremely
  important position in the evolutionary development of human cooperation" (Bowles & Gintis 2004)
  and can promote cooperation on a broader scale even when individually disadvantageous in the short
  term (Gürerk et al. 2006).
- The behavior is observable in economic-game designs (ultimatum / third-party-punishment games)
  where a "punisher" can pay a cost to reduce a violator's payoff.

## Relevance to Machine Psychology

[[sources/lei-et-al-2024]] applies altruistic punishment as a behavioral probe of LLM agents: by
placing a model in the third-party "judge" role of an unfair-allocation game, it tests whether the
model will *pay a cost* to enforce fairness — operationalizing the model's alignment with human
fairness/justice norms as a measurable rejection rate, and finding GPT-4o punishes unfairness more
readily than humans do.
