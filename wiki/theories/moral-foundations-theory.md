# Moral Foundations Theory

> [!info] In plain terms
> Why do liberals and conservatives moralize different things? This theory says human
> morality rests on a few innate "foundations" — like caring for others, fairness, loyalty to
> the group, respect for authority, and a sense of purity — and that political groups weight
> them differently (conservatives lean more on loyalty, authority, and purity). It underlies
> the questionnaire used here to probe a model's moral leanings.

## Statement

Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt 2007; Graham et al. 2009, 2011, 2013) proposes that moral
judgment is built on a small set of psychological **foundations**. The standard set comprises
five: **care/harm**, **fairness/reciprocity**, **in-group/loyalty**, **authority/respect**,
and **purity/sanctity**. (Within this source, the three foundations named verbatim are
"authority-respect, in-group-loyalty, and purity-sanctity"; the full five-foundation set is
attributed to the theory references, not enumerated by Pellert et al.)

Its central, testable prediction: political groups rely on **different subsets** of the
foundations — liberals weight care and fairness most heavily, while conservatives give
comparatively more weight to in-group/loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanctity. This
ideology-by-foundation mapping is what makes a foundation profile interpretable as
politically "left-" or "right-coded."

## Relevance to Machine Psychology

The ideology mapping turns a model's foundation profile into a readable political signal: a
model that over-weights authority, loyalty, and purity relative to a human reference group
can be characterized as leaning toward the conservative-coded foundations — which is exactly
the comparison the source draws against "the average, politically moderate American."
